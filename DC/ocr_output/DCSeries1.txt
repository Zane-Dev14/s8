1. Difference between distributed systems and distributed
programming.
1. Distributed Systems
Definition:
A Distributed System is like a group of independent computers working together to appear as
one single system to the user. Think of it as different stores in a chain acting like one big
supermarket online. Even though each store (computer) operates on its own, when you shop, it
feels like you're using a single, unified service.
Key Features:

===== PAGE 4 =====
e No Shared Memory: Each computer (or node) has its own memory. They don't directly
share information but talk to each other by sending messages.
e No Common Clock: There’s no universal clock keeping time for all the computers,
meaning they operate at their own pace.
e Geographical Spread: These systems can be spread out globally (like Google’s servers
worldwide) or locally (like a cluster of servers in a data center).
e Autonomy & Diversity: Each computer can run different software, have different speeds,
and even be used for different purposes, but they all collaborate.
Why Use Distributed Systems?
e Resource Sharing: Share data and tools that are too big or expensive to replicate
everywhere.
e Reliability: If one computer fails, others can keep things running.
e Scalability: Easily add more computers to handle more work.
e Remote Access: Get data from faraway places, like accessing a cloud server.
2. Distributed Programming: Writing Code for Distributed Systems
Definition:
Distributed Programming is the art of writing code that runs on multiple computers in a
distributed system. It’s about making sure these computers can talk to each other correctly and
work together efficiently.
Imagine this:
You're organizing a group project. Each team member (computer) works on a different part of
the project (task). Distributed programming is like setting up the communication—emails,
messages, deadlines—so everyone knows what to do and when.
Key Concepts in Distributed Programming:
e Message Passing: Computers don’t share memory directly; instead, they send messages
to share information.
e Asynchronous Execution: Computers don’t wait around for each other. They keep
working independently and process messages when they arrive.

===== PAGE 5 =====
e Events & State: Each computer goes through events like sending, receiving, or processing
data, which changes its internal state.
Challenges in Distributed Programming:

e Coordination: Ensuring computers are in sync when needed, like agreeing on the correct

data (e.g., banking transactions).

e Handling Failures: What if one computer crashes? The program should still work or

recover gracefully.

e Latency & Timing: Messages might get delayed or arrive out of order, so your program

needs to handle that.

*2. Transparency requirements***
In distributed systems, transparency means hiding the complexity of the system from users
and developers. Even though multiple computers are working together behind the scenes, it
should feel like you're interacting with a single, simple system.
1. Access Transparency
e What it means: Users shouldn't have to worry about how or where they access resources.
e Example: Whether you open a file from your computer or from Google Drive, it feels the
same—you just click and open it.
2. Location Transparency
e What it means: You don’t need to know where a resource or service is physically located.
e Example: When you visit a website, you don’t know (or care) which data center the server
is in; the website just works.
3. Concurrency Transparency
e What it means: Multiple users can use the system at the same time without interfering with
each other.
e Example: On Amazon, thousands of people can buy things at once, and no one's orders
get mixed up.
4. Replication Transparency
e What it means: The system might have multiple copies (replicas) of data to improve speed
or reliability, but you only see one version.
e Example: When you watch a YouTube video, it might come from a server near you, but you
don’t notice—it’s seamless.
5, Failure Transparency
e What it means: If a part of the system crashes or fails, you shouldn't notice any disruption.
e Example: If one of Netflix’s servers goes down while you're watching a show, the system
switches to another server without interrupting your stream.

===== PAGE 7 =====
6. Mobility Transparency
e What it means: You can move around and still access the system as if nothing changed.
e Example: Using WhatsApp on your phone while traveling—you still get your messages no
matter where you are.
7. Performance Transparency
e What it means: The system automatically adjusts to provide the best performance, and you
don’t need to manage it.
e Example: Google Search feels fast even when millions of people are searching at the same
time because it balances the load across servers.
8. Scaling Transparency
e What it means: The system can grow (add more resources) or shrink without affecting how
it works for users.
e Example: Adding more servers to a cloud service like Dropbox doesn’t change how you
upload files.
3. Causal precedence relation
Imagine you and your friends are texting in a group chat. Sometimes, messages depend on
each other (like when someone replies to a question), and sometimes they don’t (like when two
people talk about different things at the same time). Causal precedence helps us understand
which events (or messages) are connected and which ones are independent.
What is Causal Precedence?
Causal precedence tells us which events depend on each other in a distributed system (like
in a group chat with multiple people sending messages).
e If Event A happens and causes Event B, we say A = B (Ahappens before B).
e If Event A and Event B have nothing to do with each other, they are concurrent (they
happen separately).
Simple Example:

===== PAGE 8 =====
e You (Person 1): "Hey, what's up?" (Event A)
e Your Friend (Person 2): "Not much, you?" (Event B)
Here, Event B depends on Event A because your friend is replying to you. So, we say:
e A = B (Ahappens before B because B is a reply to A)
Now imagine:
e You (Person 1): "Hey, what's up?" (Event A)
e Another Friend (Person 3) at the same time: "Anyone watched the game last night?"
(Event C)
These two events have nothing to do with each other, so they are concurrent (happen
independently).
How Does It Work in Distributed Systems?
In distributed systems, computers send messages to each other just like people do in a group
chat. These messages (or events) can be connected or independent.
1. Same Process (Like talking to yourself):
e If Event A happens before Event B on the same computer, we say A = B.
2. Message Between Computers (Like texting a friend):
e If Computer 1 sends a message (Event A), and Computer 2 receives it (Event B),
then A | B.
3. Chain of Events:
e IfA = BandB | C, thenA | C.
(If A causes B, and B causes C, then A causes C.)
Logical vs. Physical Concurrency
1. Logical Concurrency (No Connection):
e Two events are logically concurrent if they don’t affect each other.
e Example: You send a text, and your friend posts on Instagram at the same time. These
actions don’t affect each other.
2. Physical Concurrency (Same Time in Real Life):
e Two events happen at exactly the same time in real life.

===== PAGE 9 =====
e Example: You and your friend both press "send" on a message at the exact same
second.

Why Does This Matter?
In distributed systems (like cloud servers, online games, or databases), knowing which events
depend on each other is super important. It helps:

e Keep data consistent (no mix-ups in messages or transactions)

e Avoid errors when multiple users are doing things at the same time

e Understand the flow of information in the system
Quick Recap:

e A = Bmeans A happened before and influenced B.

e Events with no connection are concurrent.

e Logical concurrency means events don’t affect each other, even if they happen at different

times.

e Physical concurrency means events happen at the exact same time in real life.
4, Leader election algorithm
In distributed systems (like a group of computers working together), it's important to have one
computer in charge—this is called the leader or coordinator. The Leader Election Algorithm
helps decide which computer becomes the leader.
Why Do We Need a Leader Election Algorithm?

e To choose a unique leader (only one at a time).

e If the leader fails or retires, the system runs the algorithm again to pick a new one.

e All computers must agree on who the leader is.
How Does It Work?

e Any computer (called a process) can start an election if it thinks the leader is down.

e Aprocess is either:

===== PAGE 10 =====
e Participant: Actively involved in the election.
e Non-participant: Not involved in the current election.
Types of Leader Election Algorithms
1. Ring-Based Election Algorithm
2. Bully Algorithm
1. Ring-Based Election Algorithm
Imagine computers arranged in a circle (ring). Each computer can only talk to its next
neighbor in the circle.
How It Works:
1. Starting the Election:
e Any process can start the election by sending its ID to its neighbor.
e It marks itself as a participant.
2. Passing the Message:
e When a process gets an election message, it compares the ID in the message with its
own:
e If the incoming ID is bigger: Forward the message.
e If the incoming ID is smaller and the receiver is not a participant: Replace the
message with its own ID and forward it.
e If already a participant, it just ignores smaller IDs.
3. Choosing the Leader:
e When a process receives a message with its own ID, it means it has the highest ID.
e This process becomes the leader (coordinator).
4. Announcing the Leader:
e The new leader sends an "elected" message around the ring to inform everyone.
Example (Ring-Based):
e Computers in the ring;3 + 7+.9~4-—6
1. Computer 3 starts the election and sends "3" to 7.

===== PAGE 11 =====
2. 7 compares IDs. Since 7 > 3, it forwards "7" to 9.
3.9 > 7, So it forwards "9" to 4.
4.4<9, so 4 does nothing.
5. When 9 gets the message back with its own ID, it knows it’s the leader!
2. Bully Algorithm
In this algorithm, the computer with the highest ID always wins—that's why it's called the
Bully Algorithm!
How It Works:
1. Starting the Election:
e Aprocess that thinks the leader has failed sends an election message to all
processes with higher IDs.
2. Responses:
e lIfno higher process responds, the process declares itself the leader.
e Ifa higher process responds, that process takes over the election.
3. Choosing the Leader:
e The process with the highest ID becomes the leader and sends a "coordinator"
message to all others.
4. If the Leader Fails Again:
e The process that notices the failure starts a new election.
Example (Bully Algorithm):
e Computers with IDs: 1, 2, 3, 4,5
1. Computer 3 notices the leader is down and sends election messages to 4 and 5.
2. Computer 5 responds (because it has a higher ID) and starts its own election.
3. Since 5 has the highest ID, it wins and sends a "coordinator" message to all others.
4. If 5 crashes, another process (Say 3 or 4) will start the election again.

5. Primitives for distributed communication
In a distributed system (where multiple computers or processes work together),
communication primitives are the basic building blocks that allow these processes to send
and receive messages. The two main primitives are:
e Send() : Used to send data to another process.
e Receive() : Used to receive data from another process.
How Send() and Receive() Work:
e Send(destination, data):
e destination : Who you are sending the data to.
e data: The actual message or information you want to send.
e Receive(source, buffer):
e source: Who you are expecting data from (can be anyone or a specific process).
e buffer : The space where the received data will be stored.
Buffered vs. Unbuffered Communication:

===== PAGE 13 =====
1. Buffered Communication:
e Data is first copied from the user's buffer to a temporary system buffer before being
sent over the network.
e Safer because if the receiver isn’t ready, the data is still stored temporarily.
2. Unbuffered Communication:
e Data goes directly from the user’s buffer to the network.
e Faster but riskier—if the receiver isn’t ready, the data could be lost.
Types of Communication Primitives
Communication primitives can be classified based on how they handle synchronization and
blocking.
1. Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Communication
e Synchronous Primitives:
e The sender and receiver must "handshake"—both must be ready for the message
to be sent and received.
e The Send() only finishes when the Receive() is also called and completed.
e Good for ensuring messages are properly received but can slow things down.
e Asynchronous Primitives:
e The Send() returns control immediately after copying the data out of the user
buffer, even if the receiver hasn't received it yet.
e Receiver doesn't need to be ready immediately.
e Faster, but there’s a risk the message might not be delivered right away.
ee & —___
2. Blocking vs. Non-Blocking Communication
e Blocking Primitives:
e The process waits (or blocks) until the operation (Sending or receiving) is fully done.
e Example: In a blocking Send() , the process won't continue until it knows the data
has been sent.
e Non-Blocking Primitives:
e The process immediately continues after starting the send or receive operation, even
if it’s not finished.

===== PAGE 14 =====
e It gets a handle (like a ticket) that it can use later to check if the message was
successfully sent or received.
e Useful for doing other work while waiting for communication to finish.
How Non-Blocking Communication Works (Handles & Waits)
When you use non-blocking communication, the system gives you a handle (like a reference
number) to check if the operation is complete.
1. Polling: You can keep checking in a loop to see if the operation is done.
2. Wait Operation: You can use a Wait() function with the handle, and it will block until the
communication is complete.
Example Scenarios
1. Blocking Synchronous Send Example:
e You send a file and wait until the receiver confirms they've received it before doing
anything else.
2. Non-Blocking Asynchronous Send Example:
e You send an email and immediately start working on something else, trusting that
the system will handle sending it in the background.
3. Blocking Receive Example:
e You wait by the phone until your friend calls—you won't do anything else until you get
the call.
4. Non-Blocking Receive Example:
e You keep your phone nearby while you do other tasks, checking occasionally to see if
you've missed a call.

e The past cone of an event ej includes all events that could have influenced it.
e In other words, any event ei that happened before ej and could have sent information or

Key Points:
e Causal Relationship (ei - ej ):
If ei + ej , it means event ei happened before event ej and could have influenced it.
e Information Accessibility:
All the information present at ei could have potentially been accessible to ej.
Example:

e Event ej: Your friend receives the message.
Since your friend receiving the message (ej) could only happen after you typed it (ei), ei is in

7. Design issues of distributed system
When building a distributed system (a system where different computers work together over a
network), you face several important challenges.
1. Communication
e What’s the problem?
Computers in different places need to talk to each other clearly and quickly.
e What to think about:
e Remote Procedure Call (RPC): Calling a function on another computer as if it's on
your own.
e Remote Object Invocation (ROI): Using objects from another computer like they’re
local.
e Messages vs. Streams: Should you send single messages or a continuous flow of
data?
2. Managing Processes
e What’s the problem?
You need to handle running programs (processes) across many machines.
e What to think about:
e Starting and Stopping Processes: How do you manage programs running on
different computers?

===== PAGE 18 =====
e Moving Code Around: Sometimes it’s better to move the program to where the data
is.
e Smart Agents: Programs that can move and act on their own across different
systems.
3. Naming Things
e What’s the problem?
You need a simple way to find computers, files, or services on the network.
e What to think about:
e User-Friendly Names: Easy-to-use names instead of complicated addresses.
e Moving Devices: How to keep track of mobile devices that change location.
4. Synchronization (Keeping Things in Sync)
e What’s the problem?
Making sure computers work together in a coordinated way.
e What to think about:
e Preventing Conflicts: Stopping two computers from accessing the same resource at
the same time.
e Choosing a Leader: Picking one computer to be in charge when needed.
e Matching Clocks: Making sure all computers agree on the time.
5. Storing and Accessing Data
e What’s the problem?
You need to store data in a way that’s fast and easy to access from anywhere.
e What to think about:
e Distributed File Systems: Storing files across multiple machines.
e Fast Access: Making sure data can be accessed quickly, even as the system grows.
6. Consistency and Replication
e What’s the problem?
When you copy data to multiple places (replication), you need to keep it consistent.
e What to think about:
e Keeping Data in Sync: Making sure all copies of the data are up-to-date.

===== PAGE 19 =====
e When to Update: Do you need instant updates or is it okay if they happen later?
7. Handling Failures (Fault Tolerance)
e What’s the problem?
Systems need to keep working even when something goes wrong.
e What to think about:
e Reliable Messaging: Making sure messages don't get lost.
e Recovering from Crashes: Saving progress so you can pick up where you left off if a
computer fails.
e Detecting Failures: Knowing when a computer or connection goes down.
8. Security
e What’s the problem?
Keeping data safe from hackers and unauthorized access.
e What to think about:
e Encryption: Protecting data by turning it into unreadable code unless you have the
key.
e Access Control: Making sure only the right people or systems can access resources.
e Secure Connections: Ensuring data sent over the network is safe from spying.
9. Scalability and Modularity
e What’s the problem?
The system should handle more users and data as it grows.
e What to think about:
e Spreading Workload: Distribute tasks across multiple machines.
e Using Caches: Storing frequently accessed data temporarily to speed things up.
e Breaking Things into Parts: Designing the system in small, manageable pieces that
can be updated separately.
8. Models of communication network

===== PAGE 20 =====
When computers in a distributed system talk to each other, they send messages over netwo—
The way these messages are sent and received can follow different models:
1. FIFO (First-In, First-Out)
e What it means: Messages are delivered in the same order they were sent.
e Example: Imagine you're standing in a line at a coffee shop. The first person to order is
the first to get their coffee.
e Why it’s useful: It’s predictable—messages don’t get mixed up.
2. Non-FIFO (Non-First-In, First-Out)
e What it means: Messages can arrive in any order, not necessarily the order they were
sent.
e Example: Think of tossing several letters into a mailbox. When the mailman delivers
them, they might come out in a different order than you sent them.
e Why it’s useful: It can be faster in some situations, but it’s harder to manage since the
order isn’t guaranteed.
3. Causal Ordering (CO)
e What it means: Messages are delivered in an order that respects cause-and-effect
relationships. If one message depends on another, it will be delivered afterward.
e Example: If you ask a question in an email and someone replies, causal ordering
makes sure you receive the reply after your question.
e Why it’s useful: It helps keep the logic of conversations or processes intact, which
simplifies how distributed systems work.
How These Models Relate to Each Other:
e Causal Ordering ¢ FIFO c Non-FIFO
e Causal Ordering is the strictest—it always respects cause and effect.
e FIFO ensures the order is correct but doesn’t always track cause and effect.
e Non-FIFO is the most flexible—messages can arrive in any order.

9. Global state and snapshot recording algorithm
In distributed systems, multiple computers (or processes) work together but don’t share
memory or a common clock. This makes it tricky to understand the whole system's current

===== PAGE 21 =====
state at any given time. That’s where global state and snapshot recording come in.
What is a Global State?
e Global State = The combined information about what’s happening in a// processes and
communication channels in the system at a specific time.
e Local State: Each process (computer) has its own local state, which includes its
memory, tasks it's working on, and the messages it has sent/received.
e Channel State: Each communication channel (the connection between processes)
has its state, which includes messages that have been sent but not yet received.
Why Record the Global State?
Recording the global state is important for:
1. Detecting Problems: Like finding deadlocks (when processes are stuck waiting for each
other) or checking if tasks have finished.
2. Failure Recovery: Saving the system’s state (called a checkpoint) helps restore it after a
crash.
3. System Analysis: Understanding how the system behaves for testing and verifying
correctness.
How Does Snapshot Recording Work?
To get a meaningful global snapshot, processes need to record their local states in a way that
makes sense together. Here’s how it's modeled:
1. Processes and Channels:
e Processes (p1, p2, ... pn): These are the computers or tasks in the system.
e Channels (Cij): The connections where messages are passed from process pi to
process pj.
2. Events in the System:
e Internal Events: Actions happening inside a process (like updating a variable).
e Send Events: When a process sends a message.
e Receive Events: When a process receives a message.
3. Recording a Global Snapshot:

===== PAGE 22 =====
e The goal is to capture the states of all processes and channels consistently, even
though messages are flying around at different times.
What is a Consistent Global State?
A consistent global state means:
e If amessage has been received, the snapshot should also show that it was sent.
e No message should appear as being received before it was sent (that wouldn’t make
sense!).
Challenges in Recording Snapshots:
1. No Global Clock: You can't tell all processes to take a snapshot at the exact same time.
2. Message Confusion: It’s hard to decide which messages should be included in the
snapshot—messages that are in transit can cause inconsistency.

11. Chandy Lambore algorithm
The Chandy-Lamport algorithm is a method to capture a consistent global snapshot of a
distributed system. It ensures that all processes and communication channels are recorded in a
way that reflects the true state of the system, even though processes run independently and
messages arrive at different times.
How It Works (Step-by-Step):
1. Starting the Snapshot (Marker Sending Rule):
e Aprocess (let’s say Process A) decides to start the snapshot.
e Process A immediately:
1. Records its own state (its local memory, tasks, etc.).
2. Sends a special control message called a marker through all its outgoing
channels before sending any other messages.
2. Receiving the Marker (Marker Receiving Rule):
e When another process (Say Process B) receives this marker:
e If Process B hasn’t recorded its state yet:
1. It records the incoming channel as empty (no messages counted).
2. It then records its own state.
3. Sends markers along all its outgoing channels (just like Process A did).
e If Process B has already recorded its state:
e It records the messages received on that channel between recording its state
and receiving the marker.
3. Ending the Snapshot:
e The algorithm ends when all processes have received a marker on every incoming
channel.
e Once this happens, the system has enough information to assemble a global
snapshot.

===== PAGE 31 =====
Example Scenario:
1. Process A starts the snapshot:
e Records its own state.
e Sends markers to Process B and Process C.
2. Process B receives the marker:
e It hasn’t recorded its state yet, so:
e Itrecords its state.
e Marks the channel from A to B as empty.
e Sends markers to its outgoing channels.
3. Process C receives the marker after it had already recorded its state:
e It records any messages it received between its snapshot and the marker.
When is This Useful?
e Detecting Deadlocks: Check if processes are stuck waiting on each other.
e Failure Recovery: Save system states (checkpoints) to recover after crashes.
e Debugging & Analysis: Understand how processes interact in real-time.
In simple terms, the Chandy-Lamport algorithm is like taking a group photo where everyone
pauses for a second so the picture accurately reflects who was doing what, even though they
usually work independently.
12. Rules for termination detection
What is Termination Detection?
In a distributed system, multiple computers (or processes) work together to solve a problem.
Since these processes are running independently and communicating over a network, it's hard
to tell when the entire system has finished its work.
Termination detection is the process of figuring out when all processes have finished their
tasks, and there are no messages still traveling through the network. Only then can we
say, "The system is done!"
Imagine This Scenario:

===== PAGE 32 =====
Let’s say we have three people (Process A, Process B, and Process C) working on solving a
puzzle. They pass notes to each other when they find clues (these notes are like messages).
Here’s the problem: how do they all know when the puzzle is completely solved?
Even if A finishes their part and stops, B might still be working, or a note might be on its way
from C to B. So, how do we detect that everyone has stopped working and no notes are in
transit?
Rules for Termination Detection:
1. No Freezing the Work (No Interference with Computation):
e The system should continue solving the puzzle while checking if it's done. We can't
pause everyone just to ask, "Are you finished?"
e Example: If Ais working, we shouldn't interrupt them just to ask if they’re done.
2. No New Ways of Talking (No New Communication Channels):
e The people can only send notes through the methods they already use. We can’t
suddenly give them walkie-talkies to check if they’re finished.
e Example: If A, B, and C usually pass handwritten notes, we can’t introduce phone calls
to check on them.
3. Every Note Will Arrive (Reliable Message Delivery):
e Even if there are delays, every note passed between people will eventually reach its
destination.
e Example: If C sends a note to A, even if it takes a long time, it will eventually arrive.
4. Notes Can Be Late (Finite but Unpredictable Delays):
e The notes might take different amounts of time to arrive, but they won't get lost forever.
e Example: A might send a note to B, and it could arrive quickly or take a while, but it will
get there.
5. People are Either Working or Waiting (Process States):
e At any time, a person is either active (working on the puzzle) or idle (waiting, doing
nothing).
e Example: Ais active when solving clues. A becomes idle when they have no more clues
to solve.
6. Rules for Switching Between Working and Waiting (Transition Rules):
e Active to Idle: A person can stop working when they finish their part.
e Example: B finishes their clue and stops.

===== PAGE 33 =====
e Idle to Active: A person can only start working again if they get a note from someone
else.
e Example: A was idle but starts working again when they receive a new clue from
C.
e Only Active People Can Send Notes: If you’re not working, you can’t send notes.
e Example: If Ais idle, they can't send any more messages until they become active
again.
7. Special "Are You Done?" Notes (Control Messages):
e To detect if everyone is finished, special notes (control messages) are used to ask, "Are
you done?" But these should not mess up the regular notes being passed.
e Example: Besides the puzzle clues, people pass special notes asking, "Have you
finished your clues?" without mixing them up with puzzle hints.
8. Notes Don’t Have to Be in Order (No FIFO Requirement):
e Notes sent between the same people don’t have to arrive in the order they were sent.
e Example: If A sends two notes to B, the second one might arrive before the first.
9. Sending and Receiving are Instant (Atomic Actions):
e When someone sends or receives a note, it happens completely, without half-finished
actions.
e Example: You can’t half-send a note; it’s either sent or not. Similarly, you can’t half-
receive a note.
How Does This Help in Detecting Termination?
e Goal: We want to find out when everyone has stopped working and no notes are in
transit.
e If everyone is idle and no messages are left to be delivered, we can safely say the work is
done.
Example of Termination Detection:
Let’s say Process A finishes its task and goes idle. But before declaring that everything is done,
we need to make sure:
1. B and C have also finished their tasks.
2. No messages are still traveling between A, B, and C.

===== PAGE 34 =====
Only when both conditions are true can we detect that the entire system has terminated.
