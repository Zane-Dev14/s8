1. Define Termination Detection.

===== PAGE 4 =====
What is Termination Detection?
In a distributed system, multiple computers (or processes) work together to solve a problem.
Since these processes are running independently and communicating over a network, it's hard
to tell when the entire system has finished its work.
Termination detection is the process of figuring out when all processes have finished their
tasks, and there are no messages still traveling through the network. Only then can we
say, "The system is done!"
Imagine This Scenario:
Let’s say we have three people (Process A, Process B, and Process C) working on solving a
puzzle. They pass notes to each other when they find clues (these notes are like messages).
Here’s the problem: how do they all know when the puzzle is completely solved?
Even if A finishes their part and stops, B might still be working, or a note might be on its way
from C to B. So, how do we detect that everyone has stopped working and no notes are in
transit?
Rules for Termination Detection:
1. No Freezing the Work (No Interference with Computation):
e The system should continue solving the puzzle while checking if it's done. We can't
pause everyone just to ask, "Are you finished?"
e Example: If Ais working, we shouldn't interrupt them just to ask if they’re done.
2. No New Ways of Talking (No New Communication Channels):
e The people can only send notes through the methods they already use. We can’t
suddenly give them walkie-talkies to check if they’re finished.
e Example: If A, B, and C usually pass handwritten notes, we can’t introduce phone calls
to check on them.
3. Every Note Will Arrive (Reliable Message Delivery):
e Even if there are delays, every note passed between people will eventually reach its
destination.
e Example: If C sends a note to A, even if it takes a long time, it will eventually arrive.
4. Notes Can Be Late (Finite but Unpredictable Delays):
e The notes might take different amounts of time to arrive, but they won't get lost forever.

===== PAGE 5 =====
e Example: A might send a note to B, and it could arrive quickly or take a while, but it will
get there.
5. People are Either Working or Waiting (Process States):
e At any time, a person is either active (working on the puzzle) or idle (waiting, doing
nothing).
e Example: A is active when solving clues. A becomes idle when they have no more clues
to solve.
6. Rules for Switching Between Working and Waiting (Transition Rules):
e Active to Idle: A person can stop working when they finish their part.
e Example: B finishes their clue and stops.
e Idle to Active: A person can only start working again if they get a note from someone
else.
e Example: A was idle but starts working again when they receive a new clue from
C.
e Only Active People Can Send Notes: If you’re not working, you can’t send notes.
e Example: If Ais idle, they can't send any more messages until they become active
again.
7. Special "Are You Done?" Notes (Control Messages):
e To detect if everyone is finished, special notes (control messages) are used to ask, "Are
you done?" But these should not mess up the regular notes being passed.
e Example: Besides the puzzle clues, people pass special notes asking, "Have you
finished your clues?" without mixing them up with puzzle hints.
8. Notes Don’t Have to Be in Order (No FIFO Requirement):
e Notes sent between the same people don’t have to arrive in the order they were sent.
e Example: If A sends two notes to B, the second one might arrive before the first.
9. Sending and Receiving are Instant (Atomic Actions):
e When someone sends or receives a note, it happens completely, without half-finished
actions.
e Example: You can’t half-send a note; it’s either sent or not. Similarly, you can’t half-
receive a note.
How Does This Help in Detecting Termination?
e Goal: We want to find out when everyone has stopped working and no notes are in
transit.

===== PAGE 6 =====
e If everyone is idle and no messages are left to be delivered, we can safely say the work is
done.
Example of Termination Detection:
Let’s say Process A finishes its task and goes idle. But before declaring that everything is done,
we need to make sure:
1. B and C have also finished their tasks.
2. No messages are still traveling between A, B, and C.
Only when both conditions are true can we detect that the entire system has terminated.
2. What are the basic properties of scalar time.
Imagine you have a bunch of computers (or processes) working together but not sharing a
clock. They need to agree on the order of events (like "who sent a message first?").
To do this, Lamport came up with the idea of Scalar Time in 1978.
Each process has its own logical clock — just a simple counter (like C1, C2, etc.).
Whenever something important happens (like sending or receiving a message), the process
updates its counter using two simple rules.
Rules to Update Clocks
R1. Local Event: When a process does something (like sending a message), it increases its
counter by 1.
| E.g., if Cl = 5, and process does something, it becomes C1 = 6.
R2. Message Received: When a process receives a message, it compares its own clock with
the timestamp in the message and takes the maximum + 1.
| If C2 = 3, and it gets a message with time 5, itsets C2 = max(3,5)+1 = 6.
This way, time always moves forward!
Properties of Scalar Time

===== PAGE 7 =====
1. Consistency Property
If one event happens before another (in real life), scalar time will reflect that.
2. Total Ordering
e We can use scalar timestamps to totally order events across the whole system.
e But sometimes... two events in different processes might have the same timestamp
e We use process IDs to break ties.
e Example:
e If two events have timestamp 5,
e one happened in Process 1, and the other in Process 2,
e we write their time as (5,1) and (5,2).
e Since 1< 2, we say (5,1) happened first.
e So the final timestamp is atuple: (time, process id)
3. Event Counting
Because the clock increases with each event, you can count events using the scalar clock
values.
4. No Strong Consistency
Scalar time is not perfectly accurate. It does not guarantee that if two events have different
timestamps, one definitely happened before the other. It only helps with a rough, logical
ordering.

4. Specify the issues in recording a global state.
In a distributed system (multiple computers or processes working together), a global state is
like taking a photo of the whole system at one moment. It includes:
e Each process's state (what it's doing, local variables, etc.)
e Each communication channel’s state (messages in transit)
Issue 1: Which messages should be included in the snapshot?
e Imagine
e Process A sends a message to Process B.
e Process B takes its snapshot before it receives the message.
e Question: Should that message be part of the snapshot?
- We need a Clear rule to decide:
- ls the message already in the process's state?
- Or is it “in transit" and should be recorded in the channel?
This is tricky because messages can fly across the network at any time, and we need to
capture a consistent picture.
Issue 2: When should each process take its snapshot?
Without a global clock, processes can't all take snapshots at the same moment.
So
e How do we decide when each process should capture its state?
e If they all do it at random times, the snapshot might not make sense (it might show events
out of order).
We need a coordinated way (like a special message or protocol) to tell each process when
it's the right time to take its snapshot, based on what's happening in the system.
5. Define vector time.

===== PAGE 9 =====
Imagine you have several computers (processes) working together in a distributed system.
These computers send messages, perform actions, and we want to know:
e Which event happened before another?
e Did two events happen independently, or were they causally connected?
That’s where Vector Clocks come in.
A vector clock is like a special timestamp, but instead of just one number (like in scalar
clocks), it's a list of numbers — one for each process in the system.
If there are 3 processes (P1, P2, P3), each process has a vector clock like this:
[clock_P1, clock_P2, clock_P3]
Each process keeps track of:
e Its own actions
e What it knows about the others
How Does It Work?
Let’s say we have 3 processes (P1, P2, P3).
Each one starts with a clock: [0, 0, 0]
Local event at P1:
e P1 does something.
e It updates its own entry: [1, 0, 0]
P1 sends a message to P2:
e It sends the message with its vector clock [1, 0, 0]
P2 receives the message:
e It compares the received clock [1, 0, 0] withits ownclock [0, 1, 0]
e It takes the max of each position and then adds 1 to its own position:
max([1, 0, 0], [0, 1, 0]) = [1, 1, 0]
Then increment P2’s own value:

===== PAGE 10 =====
[1, 2, 0]
Key Properties of Vector Time
1. Isomorphism (Partial Ordering)
If event A |= event B (A happened before B), then A’s vector clock < B’s vector clock
(we compare them element-by-element).
This helps us create a partial order of events in the system.
2. Strong Consistency
Unlike scalar clocks, vector clocks can tell us exactly if two events are related:
e If A's vector clock < B's, then A happened before B
e If Aand B are not comparable, they happened concurrently
3. Event Counting
Just like scalar clocks, we can count events because vector entries increase with every action.
Applications of Vector Time
Because vector clocks track causality perfectly, they’re super useful:
e Distributed debugging — trace what happened and in what order
e Causal message ordering — ensure messages are delivered respecting cause-and-effect
e Causal shared memory — maintain consistent views of shared data
e Global breakpoints — pause systems in a stable state
e Checkpoint consistency — useful in failure recovery

7. Discuss the method of termination detection by weight
throwing in detail.
We want to know when a distributed computation has ended. This can be tricky because
different processes are working independently and might still be sending messages to each
other. So we use a technique called Termination Detection by Weight Throwing to figure this
out.
Key Characters:
1. Controlling Agent — like the manager, it monitors everything.
2. Processes (P1, P2, etc.) — the workers who actually do the job.
3. Messages — ways to communicate between the controller and processes, and between
processes.
Weights (W):
e Everything starts with a total weight of 1.

===== PAGE 16 =====
e Initially, the controlling agent has all the weight (W = 1).
e All other processes start idle with weight 0.
How it works — Rules and Flow:
1. Starting the Work (Rule 1):
e The controlling agent wants a process to do some work.
e It splits its weight into two parts, say W1 and W2 (W1 + W2 = current W).
e It keeps W1, and sends W2 in a basic message B(W2) to aprocess P.
e That process now has W2 weight and is considered active.
2. When a Process Gets a Message (Rule 2):
e Suppose a process P receives a basic message B(DW) .
e It adds DW to its own weight.
e If it was idle, it becomes active.
3. Becoming Idle (Rule 3):
e Ifa process finishes its work and wants to stop, it:
e Sends acontrol message C(W) to the controlling agent.
e Sets its own weight to 0 (it's now passive).
4. When the Manager Gets Weight Back (Rule 4):
e The controlling agent receives a control message C(DW) .
e It adds DW to its own weight.
e If its total weight becomes 1 again, it means:
e No processes are active.
e No messages are in transit.
e Computation has ended!
Why this works?
e Weight Conservation: The total weight (across all processes + all messages in transit +
the controller) is always exactly 1.
e So if the controller ends up with all of it back, it means:

e No weight is left anywhere else — No ongoing work = Safe to terminate.

9. Explain in detail about Chandy Lamport algorithm.
The Chandy-Lamport algorithm is a method to capture a consistent global snapshot of a
distributed system. It ensures that all processes and communication channels are recorded in a
way that reflects the true state of the system, even though processes run independently and
messages arrive at different times.
How It Works (Step-by-Step):
1. Starting the Snapshot (Marker Sending Rule):
e Aprocess (let’s say Process A) decides to start the snapshot.
e Process A immediately:
1. Records its own state (its local memory, tasks, etc.).
2. Sends a special control message called a marker through all its outgoing
channels before sending any other messages.
2. Receiving the Marker (Marker Receiving Rule):
e When another process (Say Process B) receives this marker:
e If Process B hasn’t recorded its state yet:
1. It records the incoming channel as empty (no messages counted).
2. It then records its own state.
3. Sends markers along all its outgoing channels (just like Process A did).
e If Process B has already recorded its state:
e It records the messages received on that channel between recording its state
and receiving the marker.
3. Ending the Snapshot:
e The algorithm ends when all processes have received a marker on every incoming
channel.
e Once this happens, the system has enough information to assemble a global
snapshot.
Example Scenario:
1. Process A starts the snapshot:
e Records its own state.

===== PAGE 24 =====
e Sends markers to Process B and Process C.
2. Process B receives the marker:
e It hasn’t recorded its state yet, so:
e It records its state.
e Marks the channel from A to B as empty.
e Sends markers to its outgoing channels.
3. Process C receives the marker after it had already recorded its state:
e It records any messages it received between its snapshot and the marker.
When is This Useful?
e Detecting Deadlocks: Check if processes are stuck waiting on each other.
e Failure Recovery: Save system states (checkpoints) to recover after crashes.
e Debugging & Analysis: Understand how processes interact in real-time.
In simple terms, the Chandy-Lamport algorithm is like taking a group photo where everyone
pauses for a second so the picture accurately reflects who was doing what, even though they
usually work independently.

12. Explain how logical clock is implemented.
In distributed systems, logical clocks are used to track the order of events across different
processes — even when there's no synchronized global clock.
1. Data Structures:
Each process has:

2. Protocol (Rules) for Updating the Clock:
The protocol ensures that all clocks progress in a consistent and causally correct way.
R1 - Local Event Rule
When a process performs an event (Send, receive, or internal), it updates its local clock.
How?
e Simply increment 1c: by 1 for every event.
e Example:
e If lci = 5 and it sends a message or does some internal operation, then — 1ci =
6.
R2 - Message Rule (Global View Update)
When a process receives a message, it updates its clock based on the timestamp attached to
the message.
How?
e The sender attaches its clock value with the message.
e The receiver sets:
lci = max(lci, received timestamp) + 1
This ensures causality is preserved — the receiver’s clock is always ahead of any
message it receives.
Example:
e If P1 sends a message with timestamp = 10 and P2has lc = 8,
P2 sets lc = max(8, 10) +1 = 11.
