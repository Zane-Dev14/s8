1. Data mining and application
What is Data Mining?
Data mining is the process of extracting useful information or patterns from large sets of data.
Think of it like searching for valuable nuggets of gold in a huge pile of rocks.
It is also known as:
e Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD)
e Data analysis
e Business intelligence
e Information harvesting
Applications of Data Mining
1. Classification — Sorting data into categories.
e Example: In a loan database, classifying applicants as eligible or risky based on their
credit history, income, and other details.
2. Estimation — Predicting an unknown value based on existing data.
e Example: Estimating a student's future grades based on their past performance.
3. Prediction — Forecasting future outcomes using data.
e Example: Predicting next week's stock price based on previous trends.

===== PAGE 3 =====
4. Market Basket Analysis (Association Rule Mining) — Identifying patterns in shopping
behavior.

e Example: If a customer buys a pen and a pencil, they are likely to buy a notebook too.

5. Clustering — Grouping similar data points together when the categories are unknown.

e Example: Grouping customers with similar buying habits for targeted marketing.

2. OLAP and OLTP

1. OLTP (Online Transaction Processing): A system that handles daily transactions like
banking, online shopping, or airline bookings. It is designed for fast and frequent updates
to data, ensuring accuracy and quick responses.

2. OLAP (Online Analytical Processing): A system that is used for analyzing large
amounts of historical data to help businesses make decisions. It focuses on data trends,
summaries, and complex queries rather than quick updates. Examples include sales
forecasting and business intelligence reports.

3. Data Preprocessing
What is Data Preprocessing?
Data preprocessing is the process of preparing raw data for analysis by cleaning,
transforming, and organizing it. Since real-world data is often incomplete, inconsistent, or
contains errors, preprocessing helps improve data quality and ensures better results in data
mining.
Why is Data Preprocessing Important?
Good data leads to accurate insights. Poor-quality data can result in incorrect conclusions.
High-quality data should be:
e Accurate (free from errors)
e Complete (no missing values)
e Consistent (data format is uniform)
e Timely (up-to-date)
e Believable (trustworthy)
e Interpretable (easy to understand)
Key Steps in Data Preprocessing

===== PAGE 5 =====
1. Data Cleaning — Fixing errors and removing noise.
e Example: Filling in missing values or correcting typos in names and dates.
2. Data Integration — Combining data from multiple sources into a single dataset.
e Example: Merging customer data from an online store and a physical store into one
system.
3. Data Reduction — Making data smaller without losing important information.
e Example: Removing duplicate records or summarizing data to reduce its size.
4. Data Transformation — Converting data into a format suitable for analysis.
e Example: Normalization, which scales data values to fit within a range (e.g., 0 to 1) to
improve the performance of machine learning models.
These steps often work together to improve the quality and efficiency of data mining. For
example, data cleaning may also involve transformation, like converting all date formats to a
single standard.

A three-tier data warehouse organizes data processing into three levels: Bottom Tier
(Storage), Middle Tier (Processing), and Top Tier (User Interaction).
1. Bottom Tier — Data Storage & Management
e This is the database layer, usually a relational database (RDBMS).
e It collects data from different sources (e.g., operational databases, external systems).
e Tasks performed:
e Data extraction: Pulling data from multiple sources.
e Data cleaning & transformation: Merging and formatting data.
e Loading & refreshing: Keeping the warehouse up-to-date.

===== PAGE 7 =====
e Tools used:
e Gateways (connects applications to databases) — Examples:
e ODBC (Open Database Connection)
e JDBC (Java Database Connection)
e OLEDB (Microsoft's database linking tool)
e Metadata repository (stores details about data and structure).
2. Middle Tier - OLAP Server (Processing & Analysis)
e This layer processes and organizes data for fast retrieval.
e Uses OLAP (Online Analytical Processing) Servers, which come in two types:
1. ROLAP (Relational OLAP): Uses relational databases to store and process
multidimensional data.
2. MOLAP (Multidimensional OLAP): Uses a specialized database that directly handles
multidimensional data for faster processing.
3. Top Tier — User Interaction (Front-End Tools)
e This is the interface layer, where users access and analyze data.
e Includes query tools, reporting tools, and data mining tools to identify trends, patterns,
and predictions.
e Examples of tasks:
e Running reports (e.g., sales performance summaries).
e Performing trend analysis (e.g., predicting next quarter’s sales).

5. KDD
What is KDD?
KDD (Knowledge Discovery in Databases) is a step-by-step process of finding useful
insights and patterns in large datasets. Data Mining is just one step in this process.

===== PAGE 8 =====
KDD vs. Data Mining
e KDD is the entire process of discovering patterns and knowledge.
e Data Mining is a step within KDD that applies algorithms to extract meaningful patterns.
Steps in KDD
1. Data Cleaning — Remove errors, noise, and inconsistencies.
2. Data Integration — Combine data from different sources.
3. Data Selection — Pick relevant data for analysis.
4. Data Transformation — Convert data into a suitable format (e.g., summarizing or normalizing
values).
5. Data Mining — Use algorithms to find patterns and trends.
6. Pattern Evaluation — Identify the most useful patterns.
7. Knowledge Presentation — Show results using charts, graphs, and reports.
Alternative Names for KDD
e Data mining
e Knowledge extraction
e Data analysis
e Information harvesting
e Business intelligence
6. SRSWOR & SRSWR
What is Sampling in Data Reduction?
Sampling helps reduce the size of a large dataset by selecting a smaller representative
subset. This makes data processing faster while still Keeping important patterns intact.
Two Main Types of Simple Random Sampling
SRSWOR (Simple Random Sampling Without Replacement)

===== PAGE 9 =====
e Each selected data point is NOT returned after being picked.

e Once a data point is chosen, it cannot appear again in the sample.

e Example: Drawing lottery numbers where each number is unique.

SRSWR (Simple Random Sampling With Replacement)

e Each selected data point is returned to the dataset after being picked.

e Adata point can appear multiple times in the sample.

e Example: Rolling a die multiple times—each roll is independent of the previous one.

10. Missing data dealing method
1 Ignore the Tuple
e Skip rows with missing values.
e Works only if the missing data is small and random.
e Not ideal if missing data is common.
2 Fill Missing Values Manually
e Aperson manually fills in missing data.
e Very time-consuming and not practical for large datasets.

===== PAGE 19 =====
3 Use a Global Constant
e Replace missing values with a fixed value like "Unknown" or -.
e Problem: The model may treat "Unknown" as a meaningful category.
4 Use Mean (Average) or Median
e Replace missing values with the mean or median of that attribute.
e Example: If the average income is $56,000, missing values for income are replaced with
$56,000.
e Good when data is normally distributed.
5 Use Class-Specific Mean or Median
e Instead of the global mean, use the mean of similar groups.
e Example: If classifying customers by credit risk, use the average income of people in the
same credit category.
e More accurate than using the global mean.
6 Use the Most Probable Value (Prediction-Based)
e Predict missing values using regression, Bayesian models, or decision trees.
e Example: A decision tree predicts missing income based on other factors like age,
education, and job title.
e Best accuracy but requires more computation.
