1. List out the three major features of data warehouse.
A Data Warehouse (DW) is a centralized repository used to store large amounts of structured
data, typically collected from various sources within an organization. It is optimized for querying,
reporting, and data analysis rather than transaction processing. These are the three major
features of a data warehouse:
1. Subject-Oriented:
e Adata warehouse is designed to focus on key business subjects rather than on the
organization's ongoing operations.
e Subjects can include things like products, customers, sales, revenue, suppliers, etc.
e This structure helps organizations analyze data from specific business perspectives,
enabling insights related to key areas of interest.
2. Integrated:
e Data in a data warehouse comes from various heterogeneous sources such as
relational databases, flat files, external APIs, etc.
e The data is integrated to provide a consistent view of the information, making it easier
to analyze and report across different datasets.
e This integration helps businesses avoid silos of data and enables more comprehensive
decision-making
3. Time-Variant:
e The data stored in a data warehouse is typically time-stamped and organized by periods
such as year, quarter, month, or day.
e This feature enables historical analysis, as it allows users to look at data over time and
compare trends, changes, and patterns from past periods.
e Time-variant data provides valuable insights into historical performance and helps
with trend analysis and forecasting.

2. Describe the similarities and the differences of OLTP and
OLAP.

1. OLTP (Online Transaction Processing): A system that handles daily transactions like
banking, online shopping, or airline bookings. It is designed for fast and frequent updates
to data, ensuring accuracy and quick responses.

2. OLAP (Online Analytical Processing): A system that is used for analyzing large
amounts of historical data to help businesses make decisions. It focuses on data trends,
summaries, and complex queries rather than quick updates. Examples include sales
forecasting and business intelligence reports.

Similarities Between OLTP and OLAP

e Both are Data Processing Systems: Both OLTP and OLAP handle large volumes of data
and are part of data management systems, but they focus on different types of operations.

e Use of Databases: Both OLTP and OLAP systems rely on databases to store and manage
data.

e Business Applications: Both are used to support business processes; OLTP is used in
transactional applications, while OLAP is used for decision support and business
intelligence.

Characteristics:

e Simple Structure: The Star Schema has a simple, intuitive design where a central fact
table is connected to multiple dimension tables.

e Dimension Tables: Each dimension is represented by a single table. These tables contain
descriptive information about the data (e.g., time, item, location).

e Fact Table: The fact table is at the center of the schema, containing foreign keys
referencing each of the dimension tables. It also contains measurable facts (e.g., sales
figures, quantities sold).

Example:

tables could include information on time, items, branches, and locations.

Advantages:

e Simpler Queries: Since the schema is less complex, queries are easier to write.

e Faster Performance: The denormalized nature of the tables allows for faster read
performance, as fewer joins are required.

Disadvantages:

e Data Redundancy: Because dimension tables are not normalized, there can be some data

redundancy, which can increase storage requirements.

The multidimensional data model is a way to organize and analyze data in a structure that
allows you to view it from multiple perspectives. It's like looking at a data set from different
angles, and the most common way to represent this is through a data cube. Let's break this
down step by step in simple terms:

===== PAGE 11 =====
1. Data Cube:
e Imagine you have a cube (like a Rubik's cube) where each face represents a different
perspective of your data.
e Inthe world of data, this cube can have many dimensions. These dimensions are the
different aspects of your data that you want to track or analyze.
e For example, in a store's sales data, the dimensions might be time, item, branch, and
location.
e Adata cube is used to view the data in multiple dimensions at once.
2. Dimensions:
e Dimensions are like categories or perspectives that describe your data.
e For example:
e Time: What time period are you looking at? (Daily, monthly, quarterly, etc.)
e Item: What items are being sold?
e Location: Where are the items being sold (different cities, stores, etc.)?
e Each dimension has its own dimension table, which gives more details about that
dimension.
e For example, a dimension table for items might list the item name, brand, and type.
e Dimensions help you organize your data and let you focus on specific parts of it.
3. Facts:
e Facts are the numeric values you want to analyze. These are the actual measurements.
e Inasales data model, facts might include:
e Sales amount (how much money was earned)
e Units sold (how many items were sold)
e Budgeted amount (how much money was expected to be earned)
e These facts are typically stored in a fact table, and this table also connects to the
dimension tables using keys.
e For example, a fact table for sales might contain sales data and include links to the
time, item, and location dimensions.

data with suitable examples.List the dififerences between

OLAP (Online Analytical Processing) operations are used to manipulate and analyze data ina
multidimensional data model, making it easier to explore the data from different perspectives.
These operations allow users to interactively query and analyze data
These are the OLAP operations
1. Roll-up (Drill-up):
e What it does:
e Roll-up aggregates data by moving from a lower level of detail to a higher level.
e It can be done by navigating up a concept hierarchy or by reducing the number of
dimensions.
e How it works:
e Inalocation dimension, you might start by looking at sales by city (detailed level). By
rolling up, you move to an aggregated view by country (higher level).
e Example:
e If the data cube is organized by cities (Chicago, New York, Vancouver, Toronto),
rolling up by location would group the data by country (USA, Canada).
e Inasales data warehouse, rolling up the time dimension might aggregate sales
by quarter instead of by month.
2. Drill-down:
e What it does:
e Drill-down is the reverse of roll-up. It navigates from more general (less detailed) data
to more specific (detailed) data.
e You can drill down by descending through a concept hierarchy or adding more
dimensions to the data.
e How it works:
e In the time dimension, you might drill down from quarters to months to get more
detailed data.
e Example:

===== PAGE 15 =====
e If you have data aggregated by quarter, drilling down would break it into sales by
month, giving more detail.
3. Slice:
e What it does:
e Slice selects data from one dimension, creating a subcube.
e It is used to view data for a specific value in a particular dimension.
e How it works:
e You can select a specific slice of the data based on one dimension.
e Example:
e Inasales data cube, you might use the slice operation to look at sales for Q1
(time dimension) across all items and locations.
4. Dice:
e What it does:
e Dice defines a subcube by selecting data from multiple dimensions at once.
e It allows you to look at a more specific set of data by specifying conditions for two or
more dimensions.
e How it works:
e You can choose specific values in more than one dimension to create a subcube.
e Example:
e You might dice the sales data to view only sales for:
e Location = “Toronto” or “Vancouver”
° Time = “Q1” or “Q2”
e Item = “home entertainment” or “computer”
e This gives a subset of the data for the selected cities, time periods, and items.
5. Pivot (Rotate):
e What it does:
e Pivot is a visualization operation that rotates or changes the data axes, offering a new
perspective of the data.
e It helps in rearranging the view of data in a different format to make analysis easier or
to focus on different parts of the data.

===== PAGE 16 =====
e How it works:
e Pivoting changes how dimensions are displayed in the data view, often rotating axes.
e Example:

e If the data cube shows sales data with items on the rows and locations on the
columns, pivoting would rotate it to display locations as rows and items as
columns.

e Ina 3D data cube, pivoting might transform it into a series of 2D slices to offer a
clearer or more useful presentation.

e Location (Chicago, New York, Toronto, Vancouver)
e Time (Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4)

Differences between ROLAP, MOLAP and HOLAP.

The three main types of OLAP (Online Analytical Processing) servers—ROLAP, MOLAP, and
HOLAP—differ in how they store, manage, and process data.

1. Relational OLAP (ROLAP) Servers:

e Storage Type: ROLAP servers store data in relational or extended-relational databases
(such as SQL databases).

e Data Processing: They rely on relational database management systems (RDBMS) to
store and query multidimensional data. OLAP operations like roll-up, drill-down, and slice
are processed dynamically by executing SQL queries

2. Multidimensional OLAP (MOLAP) Servers:

e Storage Type: MOLAP servers use multidimensional data storage, typically in the form of
data cubes (array-based storage).

e Data Processing: MOLAP stores summarized data and pre-computes aggregations in
multidimensional arrays (cubes), allowing fast data retrieval and analysis.

3. Hybrid OLAP (HOLAP) Servers:

e Storage Type: HOLAP combines both ROLAP and MOLAP techniques to benefit from the
strengths of both approaches. Detailed data is stored in a relational database (ROLAP),
while aggregated data is stored in multidimensional cubes (MOLAP).

e Data Processing: HOLAP servers allow for the storage of large amounts of detailed data in
a relational database, while aggregations are kept in the MOLAP storage for faster access.

The Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD) process is a comprehensive sequence of
steps aimed at extracting useful patterns and knowledge from large datasets. Data mining is a

===== PAGE 19 =====
crucial part of this process but is just one of its stages.
1. Data Cleaning:
e Purpose: To remove noise and inconsistencies in the data.
e Actions:
e Handling missing data (e.g., through imputation or removal).
e Removing duplicates.
e Correcting errors in data (e.g., fixing inconsistencies or invalid values).
e Example: If some sales records have missing values for the product price, those rows are
cleaned up before analysis.
2. Data Integration:
e Purpose: To combine data from multiple sources into a unified view.
e Actions:
e Merging data from different databases or sources, such as combining customer data
from a CRM system with transaction data from an e-commerce platform.
e Ensuring that data from different sources match and are consistent.
e Example: If a retail company has data in separate databases for customer information,
inventory, and sales, the integration process would combine these into a single dataset.
3. Data Selection:
e Purpose: To select relevant data for analysis.
e Actions:
e Filtering the data to focus only on the data points that are necessary for the task at
hand.
e Removing irrelevant data that will not contribute to the analysis.
e Example: If the task is to analyze customer behavior, only the customer and transaction-
related data might be selected, excluding irrelevant information like employee records.
4, Data Transformation:
e Purpose: To transform and consolidate the data into a form suitable for mining.
e Actions:
e Normalization: Scaling data to a common range.

===== PAGE 20 =====
e Aggregation: Combining data to a higher level (e.g., summing daily sales into monthly
sales).
e Feature extraction: Creating new features from existing ones (e.g., deriving a “profit
margin" feature from "cost" and "price’).
e Example: If sales data is recorded daily, the transformation step could aggregate it into
monthly data for easier analysis.
5. Data Mining:
e Purpose: To apply intelligent methods and algorithms to extract patterns and knowledge
from the data.
e Actions:
e Use data mining techniques such as classification, clustering, regression, and
association rule mining.
e It’s the core process where patterns are discovered through algorithms.
e Example: Using clustering to group customers based on purchasing behavior or applying
decision trees to predict customer churn.
6. Pattern Evaluation:
e Purpose: To evaluate and identify the truly interesting patterns.
e Actions:
e Assess the discovered patterns using interestingness measures, which could include
factors like novelty, usefulness, and validity.
e Identify patterns that offer actionable insights or represent significant knowledge.
e Example: Evaluating the strength of association rules like "If a customer buys product A,
they are 80% likely to buy product B," and deciding if the rule is worth further exploration.
7. Knowledge Presentation:
e Purpose: To present the discovered patterns in a user-friendly manner.
e Actions:
e Visualizing the results through charts, graphs, or dashboards.
e Using techniques like decision trees, heat maps, and summary reports to
communicate the findings to stakeholders.

===== PAGE 21 =====
e Example: Displaying a bar chart of customer segments based on purchasing behavior or a
decision tree showing factors that influence loan approvals.

10. Describe different issues in data mining

===== PAGE 24 =====
Data mining, which involves extracting useful patterns from large datasets, comes with a variety
of challenges. These challenges can be grouped into three main categories: Mining
Methodology and User Interaction, Performance Issues, and Diversity of Database Types.
1. Mining Methodology and User Interaction Issues

1.1 Mining Different Kinds of Knowledge in Databases

e Problem: Different users want different types of knowledge. For example, a sales manager
may be interested in customer buying patterns, while a marketing team might want to know
about trends in advertising effectiveness.

e Challenge: Data mining needs to be flexible enough to handle many types of analysis. The
system should allow for different techniques to be applied depending on what the user
wants to discover.

1.2 Interactive Mining at Multiple Levels of Abstraction

e Problem: Users often need to explore data at different levels, from high-level trends to very
specific details.

e Challenge: The system should allow users to interactively explore the data at various levels
of detail. For example, a user might start by looking at sales by year, and then drill down to
see sales by month, then day, and so on.

1.3 Incorporation of Background Knowledge

e Problem: Sometimes, users have prior knowledge about the data or the domain they are
working in. For instance, a business analyst may know that certain products sell better in
certain seasons.

e Challenge: It’s important to use this background knowledge to guide the data mining
process and to help express the discovered patterns in a meaningful way.

1.4 Data Mining Query Languages and Ad-hoc Mining

e Problem: Users often need to define specific, one-time queries to mine data, which may not
fit into predefined templates.

e Challenge: A flexible query language should allow users to describe their specific data
mining tasks. This would help users ask more customized questions without needing
technical expertise.

1.5 Presentation and Visualization of Data Mining Results

===== PAGE 25 =====
e Problem: After patterns are discovered, they need to be presented in a way that is easy to
understand, especially for non-technical users.

e Challenge: The system must provide clear visual representations (charts, graphs, etc.) to
communicate the findings effectively. These representations should be simple and intuitive.

1.6 Handling Noisy or Incomplete Data

e Problem: Real-world data is often messy—there could be errors or missing values in the
dataset.

e Challenge: Effective data cleaning techniques must be applied to handle noisy (incorrect or
inconsistent) or incomplete data. If this is not done well, the patterns discovered may be
inaccurate or misleading.

1.7 Pattern Evaluation

e Problem: Not all discovered patterns are useful. Some may be obvious, while others might
be too complex or irrelevant.

e Challenge: It is important to evaluate the patterns based on their usefulness and novelty. A
pattern that’s already well-known may not be interesting, while new, actionable patterns are
valuable.

2. Performance Issues
2.1 Efficiency and Scalability of Data Mining Algorithms

e Problem: Databases can be very large, and running data mining algorithms on huge
datasets can take a long time.

e Challenge: Algorithms need to be efficient so that they can handle large volumes of data
quickly. The speed of the algorithms should be predictable, ensuring that users don’t have
to wait too long for results.

2.2 Parallel, Distributed, and Incremental Mining Algorithms

e Problem: Large datasets are often distributed across multiple machines, and the mining
process can become slower if done sequentially.

e Challenge: Parallel and distributed algorithms divide the data into parts and process them
at the same time across different machines. This helps speed up the mining process.
Additionally, incremental algorithms can update results as new data arrives, improving
efficiency.

===== PAGE 26 =====
3. Issues Relating to the Diversity of Database Types
3.1 Handling Relational and Complex Types of Data

e Problem: Data comes in many forms: some data is simple (like numbers in tables), while
other data types are complex, like images, videos, or even geographical information.

e Challenge: Different types of data require different mining methods. A single mining system
may not be able to handle all types of data, so specialized systems are needed for each
type (e.g., image mining, spatial data mining).

3.2 Mining Information from Heterogeneous Databases

e Problem: Data is often spread across different sources, and these sources may not all have
the same structure. For example, some data might be in a relational database, while other
data might be in a text file or even in a non-traditional format like social media data.

e Challenge: Mining data from heterogeneous sources (databases, web, etc.) can be difficult
because the data may be structured differently (Structured, semi-structured, unstructured). A
unified approach is needed to handle and integrate this data effectively.

14, List and explain various data mining functionalities.
1. Data Characterization:

e What it is: This involves summarizing the main features or characteristics of a specific
group of data.

e Example: If you're studying students' grades, data characterization might involve
summarizing the average grade, highest grade, and common trends of all students who
passed a particular exam.

2. Data Discrimination:

e What it is: This involves comparing the features of a target class (group) of data with those
of another group (contrasting class).

e Example: If you want to compare students who passed an exam with those who failed, you
would look at the characteristics (like study hours, attendance) to highlight the differences
between these two groups.

3. Classification:

e What it is: Classification is the process of sorting data into predefined categories or classes
based on their characteristics.

e Example: Imagine classifying emails as "Spam" or "Not Spam" based on features like
subject line, sender, and content. Classification can be done using algorithms like decision
trees or Naive Bayes.

e Usage: It's useful for organizing information and predicting unknown data.

4. Prediction:

===== PAGE 35 =====
e What it is: Prediction involves forecasting the value of something (like sales or weather)
based on existing data.

e Example: Predicting next month's sales based on historical sales data or forecasting the
weather using temperature patterns from the past.

e Usage: It's used for continuous values, like predicting stock prices or house prices.

5. Clustering:

e What it is: Clustering groups similar data together, but unlike classification, there are no
predefined labels for these groups.

e Example: Grouping customers based on their buying behavior, without knowing beforehand
how many groups (clusters) there will be. Customers who shop similarly will be grouped
together.

e Usage: Common in image processing, pattern recognition, and market segmentation.

6. Association Rule Mining:

e What it is: This involves finding relationships or patterns between items in a dataset.

e Example: A well-known example is market basket analysis: "If a customer buys bread,
they often buy butter."

e Usage: Used to understand the connection between different items and behaviors, helpful
in recommendations and marketing strategies.

7. Outlier Analysis:

e What it is: This focuses on identifying data points that don’t fit well with the general trends
or patterns in the dataset.

e Example: A student who scored much higher or lower than all others in an exam could be
an outlier. These unusual data points can provide valuable insights or indicate errors in
data.

e Usage: Important in fraud detection, anomaly detection, and quality control.

8. Evolution Analysis:

e What it is: This analyzes how data changes over time to find trends and patterns.

e Example: Tracking how the popularity of certain products changes over different seasons or
years.

===== PAGE 36 =====
e Usage: Helpful in trend analysis, like predicting market demand or understanding long-term
customer behavior.
